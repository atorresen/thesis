These wires were folded by makers of math and art.


In "make art with math" workshops -- in math classrooms and in art classrooms, in an art museum and in a math department, with artists and mathematicians from ages 4 to 79 -- over 200 participants collaborated to fold the first several thousand digits of the Kolakoski sequence into wire.


The Kolakoski sequence (1, 2, 2, 1, 1, 2, 1, 2, 2, 1, 2, 2, 1, 1, ...) was proposed by the artist William Kolakoski, who was a trained painter and a recreational mathematician.


The Kolakoski sequence is an infinitely long list constructed entirely from the number 1, the number 2, and a simple rule: the sequence alternates between blocks of 1s and blocks of 2s whose lengths are defined by the 1s and 2s in the sequence.


The Kolakoski sequence is self-referential and self-generating; it defines itself by describing itself.


The rule that defines the Kolakoski sequence is best felt by building it with your own hands. The wires assembled here are artifacts of learning/doing/making/touching/feeling this beautiful little piece of math.



I invite you to see it and feel it and sit in it and breathe it in and hold it in your lap.
Art isn’t too scary, and neither is math.
