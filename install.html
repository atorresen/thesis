These wires were folded by makers of math and art.


In "make art with math" workshops, in an art museum and in a math department, in math classrooms and in art classrooms, with people from ages 12 to 72, we bounced between math and art and math as we did art/math to make art.


The Kolakoski sequence (1, 2, 2, 1, 1, 2, 1, 2, 2, 1, 2, 2, 1, 1, ...) was proposed by the artist William Kolakoski, who was a trained painter and a recreational mathematician.

The Kolakoski sequence is an infinitely long list constructed entirely from the number 1, the number 2, and a simple rule.

The sequence alternates between blocks of 1s and blocks of 2s whose lengths are defined by the 1s and 2s within the sequence. The sequence is self-referential and self-generating; it defines itself by describing itself.

The rhythms that propel the Kolakoski sequence forward are best felt by building them with your own hands. The wires assembled here are artifacts of tactile experiences of learning/doing/making/feeling this beautiful little piece of math.

I invite you to let it flow over you. I invite you to see it and feel it and sit in it and hold it in your lap. Don’t worry. Art isn’t too scary, and neither is math.
